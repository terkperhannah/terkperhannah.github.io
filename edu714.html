Context: Math 15

Welcome to my final project for EDU 714: Course Design. For this project, I evaluated Math 15, a foundational math course tailored to support students in improving their numeracy skills and developing the skills needed to be successful in high school math.

The Learners

The branch of Math 15 I taught was designed for English Language Learners (ELLs) aged 18–20 new to Canada. Math 15 was the first class I taught after graduating from university, and it holds special significance in my teaching journey.

The Goal

Using Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles, I sought to create an inclusive and engaging course environment that prioritizes learner variability, allowing students to progress at their own pace.

This analysis is informed by key readings, including Bastoni et al. (2023), who emphasize the importance of professional development in implementing UDL strategies, and Gentile & Budzilowicz (2022), who highlight the value of culturally responsive pedagogy.

The ADDIE Model Integration

A systematic instructional design framework applied to redesigning Math 15.

Phase 1: Analysis

The Analysis phase involved assessing the target audience—English Language Learners (ELLs) aged 18–20 who are new to Canada. Many have interrupted schooling backgrounds and require foundational numeracy skills before advancing to high school math.

  • Identified language barriers as the primary obstacle to math comprehension.
  • Assessed current skill levels through initial diagnostics.
  • Recognized the need for culturally responsive pedagogy and flexible pacing.

Phase 2: Design

The Design phase utilized Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles to outline learning objectives and instructional strategies. The course was mapped out to be self-paced, providing learner autonomy.

  • Designed a self-paced booklet structure for clear progression.
  • Planned for multiple means of engagement (1-on-1, small groups).
  • Outlined integration points for visual aids and translated resources.

Phase 3: Development

This phase focused on creating the actual course materials and multimedia resources necessary to support the diverse needs identified in the Analysis phase.

Materials Created

Practice booklets, translated glossaries, and step-by-step visual diagrams.

Multimedia

Curating video tutorials to provide multiple means of representation.

Phase 4: Implementation

Delivering the course to the students while fostering a collaborative and supportive environment. This included gamified activities and active peer learning.

  • Executing collaborative problem-solving on digital/physical whiteboards.
  • Facilitating gamified learning (e.g., Kahoot, educational games).
  • Providing real-time scaffolding and peer-tutoring opportunities.

Phase 5: Evaluation

The final phase involves evaluating the course's effectiveness against UDL guidelines and making iterative recommendations for improvement.

  • Assessing formative and summative success rates.
  • Utilizing UDL Checklists to identify gaps in representation and expression.
  • Formulating recommendations (e.g., project-based assessments) for future iterations.

UDL Course Evaluation

Evaluating Math 15 through the three core principles of Universal Design for Learning, and identifying actionable recommendations.

Why UDL?

By embedding UDL insights, the project aims to bridge gaps in course design and align Math 15 with the broader goals of inclusive education (Sewell et al., 2022).

Engagement

Current Evaluation

The self-paced structure fosters student autonomy. However, it can leave some learners disengaged without timely support. One-on-one teaching is used, but there's a lack of structured peer interaction.

Recommendations

Incorporate gamified activities (Kahoot, Quizlet Live) and schedule weekly group problem-solving sessions. This fosters collaboration and community, especially beneficial for ELLs (Gentile & Budzilowicz, 2022).

Representation

Current Evaluation

Practice booklets ensure consistent delivery, but relying on a single medium doesn't address the diverse needs of ELLs.

Recommendations

  • Video tutorials for step-by-step explanations.
  • Visual aids (diagrams, charts) to simplify concepts.
  • Translated materials and glossaries to bridge language barriers.

Action & Expression

Current Evaluation

Evaluations are primarily unit exams. This provides standardized measures but doesn't account for varied ways students can demonstrate learning.

Recommendations

  • Project-based assessments: Apply math to real-world scenarios.
  • Self-assessment tools: Encourage metacognition.
  • Build support structures like drop-in sessions and peer-mentoring.
Implementation

Collaborative Math in Action

Collaboration is important for effective learning, especially in foundational courses like Math 15. Collaborative activities allow students to build communication skills, share problem-solving strategies, and gain confidence.

These opportunities are particularly valuable for ELLs, who often benefit from peer interactions that reinforce both math concepts and language acquisition (Bastoni et al., 2023).
