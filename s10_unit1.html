Science 10 – Energy & Matter in Chemical Change

🔬 Unit A: Energy & Matter in Chemical Change

Atoms, ions, chemical reactions, and the conservation of mass — the foundation of modern chemistry.

From the atomic model to balancing complex reactions, this unit explores how substances interact, how energy flows, and how we classify matter. Students will investigate ionic and molecular compounds, the periodic table, IUPAC nomenclature, and the law of conservation of mass.

🧪 Atomic theory ⚛️ Periodic table trends 🔁 Balancing equations 🧴 Ionic & molecular compounds 🌡️ Exothermic/Endothermic ⚖️ Mole concept

📖 Alberta Program of Studies

🔍 Key STS & Knowledge outcomes: Students will describe the basic particles that make up matter, explain how elements combine using the periodic table, classify chemical changes, write balanced equations, and apply the mole concept to real-world quantities.

  • Atomic models: Trace evidence from Dalton, Thomson, Rutherford, Bohr – how our view of the atom evolved.
  • IUPAC naming: Predict formulas and name ionic/molecular compounds and common acids using periodic table and ion tables.
  • Classify reactions: Synthesis, decomposition, single/double replacement, hydrocarbon combustion.
  • Balanced equations & conservation of mass: Translate word equations to symbolic form; verify Lavoisier's law.
  • Mole & Avogadro: Relate 6.02 × 10²³ particles to measurable quantities, calculate moles of substances.
  • Energy changes: Differentiate endothermic vs exothermic reactions (e.g., photosynthesis, combustion).
  • Safety & WHMIS: Recognize safe handling of laboratory chemicals, hazardous compounds, MSDS.
💡 Did you know? First Nations communities historically used biotic and abiotic materials (like brain tissue for tanning) based on chemical understanding of tanning agents — an example of applied chemistry!

🧴 The Mole & Avogadro's Number – Real-world Quantities

⚖️ Molar mass & counting atoms: 1 mole = 6.022 × 10²³ particles (atoms, molecules, ions). This number allows chemists to weigh out substances. For water (H₂O), 1 mole = 18.02 g, containing 6.02×10²³ molecules.

✏️ Example problem: How many moles in 36.04 g of water? → 36.04 g / 18.02 g/mol = 2.00 moles. Balanced equations can be interpreted in terms of moles!

✨ Relating to Lavoisier: Mass is conserved in reactions, moles reflect particle ratios.
