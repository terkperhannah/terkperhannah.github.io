Assistive Technology Implementation Plan

Context & Equity Challenge

I am an educator in Edmonton, Alberta, teaching in a secondary school (Grades 7–12) with a diverse student population. Our community includes multilingual learners (ELL), students with identified and unidentified learning needs, and students from historically marginalized communities (Kiritsis & Manolis, 2024).

My teaching background includes math and computer science, and I regularly integrate technology into instruction. Over the past four years, my technology use has evolved from simple LMS posting to building interactive virtual classroom extensions that align with Universal Design for Learning principles (CAST, n.d.).

The Digital Divide

A key equity reality in my context is that students use family-purchased personal laptops. Device quality varies widely—from high-performance machines to entry-level devices that struggle with multitasking, a symptom of the "Digital Access Divide" (U.S. Department of Education, 2024).

Inclusive Design

"Everyone has a computer" does not mean everyone has equal access (Miah, 2023). I aim to choose tools that reduce barriers for reading load, processing speed, and executive functioning without relying on high-end hardware.

My Goal

"To use frameworks like SETT, UDL, TPACK, and SAMR to make technology choices that reduce barriers and support student confidence and independence—without relying on tools that only work for students with stronger devices or more resources."

The SETT Framework Analysis

A systematic approach to selecting Assistive Technology (AT) by analyzing the Student, Environment, Tasks, and Tools.

Learner Profile

Target Audience: Grade 10-12 Math Students

Demographics: Diverse classroom including ELLs, students with ADHD, and students with varied processing speeds (Madhumathi et al., 2024).

Strengths

  • Comfortable with basic technology.
  • Visual learners who benefit from examples.
  • Collaborative when given structure.

Needs & Challenges

  • Executive Functioning: Difficulty organizing digital files and tracking deadlines (Mosher et al., 2022).
  • Processing Load: High cognitive load when switching between note-taking and listening.
  • Variable Access: Stress related to laggy laptops affecting participation (U.S. Dept of Education, 2024).

Physical

BYOD classroom. High variability in hardware quality. Limited power outlets. Reliable high-speed Wi-Fi available.

Social

Collaborative seating. Culture of peer support, but some stigma around "special" accommodations. Goal: Universal design.

Instructional

Blended learning model. Microsoft Teams & Google Sites. Emphasis on process over final answers. Fast-paced.

Key Tasks for Success

What do we need the students to be able to do?

Academic Tasks

  • • Access and read complex mathematical text/problems.
  • • Organize digital notes and resources efficiently.
  • • Demonstrate understanding through written work.

Functional Tasks

  • • Navigate the learning management system (LMS).
  • • Manage time during independent work blocks.
  • • Collaborate on shared digital whiteboards.

Tool Selection Criteria

Tools must be platform-agnostic (web-based), low-bandwidth friendly, and integrate with existing ecosystems (Almufarreh & Arshad, 2023).

EquatIO

Math Input

Allows students to speak, draw, or type math equations. Essential for dysgraphia.

Rationale: Reduces barrier of "typing math" (CAST, n.d.).

Immersive Reader

Text-to-Speech

Reads text aloud, highlights lines. Helps ELL and reading challenges (Kaczorowski et al., 2023).

Rationale: Universal tool, no extra install.

Desmos Classroom

Interactive

Web-based graphing and activity builder. Real-time feedback.

Rationale: Runs on any device.

Action Plan

Implementation Strategy

A phased approach to integrating Assistive Technology, ensuring sustainability.

Phase 1: Preparation & Training

Stakeholder Engagement

  • Students: Survey for barriers (National Equity Project, 2024).
  • Parents: Newsletter on equity vs equality (Ott, 2017).
  • IT Support: Whitelisting tools.

Resource Creation

Create short "How-to" videos for accessing tools. Post on LMS to support independence (CAST, 2024).

Phase 2: Step-by-Step Rollout

Month 1: Universal Introduction
Introduce Immersive Reader to the entire class as a proofreading tool, not just an accommodation. This reduces stigma.
Month 2: Targeted Math Tools
Introduce EquatIO during a low-stakes assessment. Allow students to choose paper or digital.
Month 3: Student Autonomy
Students create their own "Tech Toolkit" plan. They self-select tools that worked best.

Phase 3: Monitoring & Sustainability

Metrics

  • Completion rates of homework.
  • Quality of written explanations.
  • Student self-efficacy scores.

Sustainability

Appoint student "Tech Captains" to help peers troubleshoot (National Equity Project, 2024).

Critical Self-Reflection

Examining the journey of designing for equity.

Professional Growth

Through this project, I've moved from viewing accommodations as "exceptions" to "essential design features." I learned that designing for the margins creates a better environment for everyone (Curb Cut Effect). This aligns with the belief that technology requires intentional training (Sesan, 2020).

Addressing Challenges

The biggest obstacle was device variability. I addressed this by strictly filtering for browser-based, lightweight tools. This constraint forced creative solutions, moving from "equal" access to "equitable" classrooms (Smith, 2023).

Connection to Course Goals

This implementation plan directly applies EDU 887 principles by moving beyond "access" to "equity".

The SETT framework provided the structure to stop chasing "shiny tools" and analyze student needs (Zabala, n.d.). This shift defines my future leadership (Kiritsis & Manolis, 2024).
