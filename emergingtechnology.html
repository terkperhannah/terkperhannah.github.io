1Context Introduction & Equity Challenge

Educational Context

Setting: STEM-focused junior high and high school in Edmonton, Alberta. The student population is diverse and includes multilingual learners, students with identified and unidentified learning needs, and students from historically marginalized communities.

Technology Context: All students have access to a personal computer; however, devices are family-purchased. This creates a wide range of device capability across the classroom. Some students have high-performance laptops that can run multiple programs smoothly, while others use entry-level devices with limited processing power, storage, and battery life. As a result, students do not experience technology access in the same way even when “everyone has a laptop.”

Equity Challenge

A key equity challenge in this context is ensuring meaningful access to learning in mathematics and robotics when students face barriers related to abstract reasoning, visual-spatial processing, reading comprehension, sustained attention, and executive functioning. These challenges are amplified when technology tasks assume consistent device performance, reliable software compatibility, or the ability to multitask digitally without lag or crashes.

In addition, limited dedicated funding for specialized assistive technologies means many supports must be addressed through intentional instructional design and strategic use of low-, mid-, and high-tech tools that are realistic for mixed-capability devices.

Impact on Learning

These barriers can limit student participation, independence, and confidence, particularly in STEM learning where success is often tied to speed, accuracy, and procedural fluency. Without intentional supports, students may disengage or internalize the belief that they are “not good at math” or “not technical,” which impacts achievement and self-efficacy.

2 SETT Framework Application

The SETT Framework

The SETT Framework organizes assistive technology considerations into four key areas: Student, Environment, Tasks, and Tools. This systematic approach ensures technology selections are purposeful, personalized, and pedagogically sound.

Student

Strengths
  • Strong verbal communication skills
  • Excellent conceptual understanding of science principles
  • High motivation and persistence
  • Effective problem-solving abilities
Challenges & Needs
  • Fine motor control limitations affecting equipment manipulation
  • Visual processing difficulties with small text and detailed graphics
  • Need for alternative measurement and data collection methods
  • Requirement for multimodal content representation
Preferences
  • Hands-on learning with appropriate supports
  • Clear, high-contrast visual displays
  • Voice-controlled or simplified interfaces
  • Collaborative learning opportunities

Environment

Physical Environment
  • Standard science lab with traditional equipment
  • Limited accessible workstations
  • Inadequate lighting for students with visual challenges
  • Crowded workspace limiting mobility
Social Environment
  • Supportive peer culture
  • Collaborative learning encouraged
  • Some awareness of accessibility needs
  • Opportunities for peer mentoring
Instructional Environment
  • Traditional lab instruction with limited adaptations
  • Minimal assistive technology integration
  • Teacher open to innovation but lacks training
  • Curriculum flexibility for accommodations

Tasks

Academic Tasks
  • Precise measurement using graduated cylinders and pipettes
  • Microscope slide preparation and observation
  • Data collection and recording during experiments
  • Graph creation and interpretation
  • Lab report writing and documentation
Functional Tasks
  • Equipment setup and calibration
  • Safe handling of lab materials
  • Time management during multi-step procedures
  • Collaboration and communication with lab partners
  • Organization of data and observations

Tools

Recommended Assistive Technologies
  • Digital Microscopes: High-contrast, zoomable displays with image capture
  • Electronic Measurement Tools: Digital calipers, scales, and graduated cylinders with voice output
  • Graphing Software: Accessible data visualization tools with screen reader compatibility
  • Voice-to-Text Software: For lab report documentation
  • Adapted Lab Equipment: Larger handles, stabilized bases, simplified controls
Selection Rationale

Tools were selected based on: 1) Alignment with specific student needs, 2) Compatibility with existing lab environment, 3) Evidence of effectiveness in similar contexts, 4) Cost-effectiveness and sustainability, 5) Ease of use with minimal training

3 Implementation Plan

Implementation Overview

A phased, evidence-based approach to assistive technology integration focusing on preparation, gradual implementation, and continuous improvement to ensure sustainable success.

Phase 1: Preparation (Weeks 1-4)

Stakeholder Training
  • Professional development for science teachers on AT principles and tools
  • Student orientation sessions for target learners
  • Parent information meetings and consent process
  • IT support staff training on maintenance and troubleshooting
Environment Preparation
  • Create dedicated accessible workstation in science lab
  • Install necessary software and hardware
  • Develop visual supports and reference materials
  • Establish equipment checkout and maintenance protocols
Customization

Individualize tool settings based on student assessment results and preferences

Phase 2: Implementation Timeline

Week 5-6: Introduce digital microscopes and adapted measurement tools during structured practice sessions
Week 7-8: Integrate AT into regular lab activities with peer support system
Week 9-10: Implement graphing software and data visualization tools
Week 11-12: Full integration with voice-to-text for lab reports
Ongoing: Weekly check-ins, adjustments, and support sessions
Progress Milestones
  • Week 4: Complete all stakeholder training
  • Week 6: Students independently use basic AT tools
  • Week 10: AT integrated into 75% of lab activities
  • Week 12: Formal mid-implementation review

Practical Implementation Strategies

Small Group Stations

Rotate AT tools through 15-minute stations with defined student roles (Operator, Recorder, Analyst)

Peer Support System

Train peer mentors to assist with AT setup and basic troubleshooting

Differentiated Tasks

Create tiered lab activities that accommodate varying levels of AT proficiency

4 Monitoring & Evaluation

Evaluation Framework

Multi-method assessment approach measuring both quantitative outcomes and qualitative experiences to ensure AT implementation effectively addresses equity challenges.

Evaluation Methods

Quantitative Measures
  • Academic Performance: Lab report scores, quiz results, project grades
  • Participation Metrics: Frequency of lab participation, contribution levels
  • Skill Development: Pre/post assessments of measurement and observation skills
  • Efficiency Measures: Time to complete lab tasks, error rates
Qualitative Measures
  • Student Interviews: Semi-structured discussions about AT experience
  • Teacher Observations: Structured observation protocols
  • Work Samples: Analysis of lab reports and data collections
  • Focus Groups: Small group discussions with stakeholders

Data Collection & Analysis

Data Collection Schedule
Time Point Data Collected Method
Baseline (Week 1) Pre-assessment, demographic data Surveys, skill tests
Mid-point (Week 6) Progress check, satisfaction Interviews, observations
Final (Week 12) Outcomes, recommendations Post-assessment, focus groups
Success Indicators
  • ≥80% of target students show improved lab participation
  • ≥70% reduction in requests for alternative assignments
  • Positive student feedback on AT usefulness (≥4/5 rating)
  • Teacher reports increased confidence in AT integration

5 Stakeholder Involvement

Collaborative Approach

Meaningful engagement of all stakeholders ensures buy-in, appropriate implementation, and sustainable success. Each group plays a distinct but interconnected role in the AT implementation process.

Stakeholder Group Role & Responsibilities Engagement Strategies Communication Methods
Students Primary users, provide feedback, participate in training Regular check-ins, choice in tool selection, peer mentoring Individual meetings, feedback forms, classroom discussions
Teachers Implementation leads, provide instruction, monitor progress Professional development, coaching sessions, planning meetings Weekly team meetings, email updates, shared documentation
Parents/Caregivers Support home practice, reinforce skills, provide insights Information sessions, home activity suggestions, progress updates Newsletters, parent-teacher conferences, digital portal
Administrators Resource allocation, policy support, program evaluation Planning committees, progress reviews, budget meetings Formal reports, presentation updates, strategic planning sessions
Specialists
(OT, SLP, AT Specialists)		 Assessment, training, technical support, adaptation design Consultation sessions, co-teaching opportunities, staff training Direct consultation, written recommendations, team meetings
IT Support Technical maintenance, software updates, troubleshooting Regular maintenance checks, emergency support, system updates Help desk tickets, scheduled maintenance, training sessions

Collaboration Strategies

  • Monthly Stakeholder Meetings: Cross-functional team reviews progress and addresses challenges
  • Shared Decision-Making: Students and families participate in tool selection and goal setting
  • Co-Teaching Model: Classroom teachers collaborate with specialists during implementation
  • Peer Support Networks: Students trained to assist classmates with AT use
  • Community Partnerships: Local organizations provide resources and expertise

Feedback Mechanisms

Structured Feedback Channels
  • Weekly student reflection journals
  • Bi-weekly teacher implementation logs
  • Monthly parent surveys
  • Quarterly stakeholder focus groups
Responsive Adjustments

Feedback is reviewed within 48 hours, with adjustments implemented within one week when feasible. Major concerns trigger immediate review and response.

6 Sustainability Plan

Long-Term Success Strategy

Comprehensive sustainability planning addresses financial, technical, and human resource considerations to ensure assistive technology implementation continues beyond initial funding and support.

Financial Sustainability

Funding Strategy
  • Initial Investment: School technology budget ($5,000)
  • Grants: Apply for educational technology and accessibility grants
  • Community Partnerships: Local business sponsorships
  • Parent-Teacher Association: Annual fundraising allocation
Cost Management
  • Prioritize multi-use tools with long lifespan
  • Implement phased purchasing over 3 years
  • Explore bulk purchasing with other schools
  • Consider open-source software alternatives

Technical Sustainability

Maintenance Plan
  • Quarterly equipment inspections and calibration
  • Annual software updates and license renewals
  • Backup system for critical tools
  • Inventory management system with replacement schedule
Technical Support
  • Trained student tech team for basic troubleshooting
  • Dedicated IT staff hours for AT support
  • Vendor support contracts for complex issues
  • Online knowledge base and troubleshooting guides

Capacity Building

Professional Development
  • Annual AT training for all science teachers
  • New teacher orientation includes AT component
  • Peer coaching and mentoring program
  • Online learning modules for ongoing skill development
Knowledge Transfer
  • Document implementation procedures and lessons learned
  • Create video tutorials and user guides
  • Establish AT committee for ongoing oversight
  • Develop succession plan for key leadership roles

Anticipated Challenges & Solutions

Potential Challenge Likelihood Impact Mitigation Strategy
Budget constraints limiting tool acquisition High Medium Prioritize essential tools, seek alternative funding, implement phased approach
Staff turnover affecting implementation continuity Medium High Comprehensive documentation, mentor system, institutionalize training
Technology obsolescence Medium Medium Regular technology reviews, budget for updates, select flexible systems
Resistance to change from stakeholders Low High Early and ongoing engagement, demonstrate benefits, provide support

7 Critical Self-Reflection

Reflection Framework

This reflection examines personal growth, implementation challenges, and connections to course learning, following the rubric's emphasis on metacognition and professional development.

Personal Growth & Learning

Professional Practice Insights

Designing this implementation plan deepened my understanding of the systematic approach required for effective assistive technology integration. I learned that successful AT implementation is not just about selecting the right tools, but about creating a comprehensive ecosystem of support that includes stakeholder engagement, ongoing training, and continuous evaluation. The SETT Framework provided a valuable structure for ensuring all aspects of the student's learning context were considered.

Equity & Accessibility Understanding

This project transformed my understanding of educational equity from a theoretical concept to a practical framework for action. I now see accessibility not as an add-on or accommodation, but as a fundamental design principle that benefits all learners. The process of identifying specific barriers and matching them with appropriate technologies revealed how seemingly small adjustments can create significant opportunities for participation and success.

Challenges & Problem-Solving

Key Implementation Challenges
  1. Balancing Individualization with Scalability: Creating solutions that meet specific student needs while remaining feasible for classroom implementation
  2. Addressing Resource Limitations: Developing a realistic plan within typical school budget constraints
  3. Ensuring Sustainable Integration: Moving beyond initial implementation to create lasting change
  4. Measuring Meaningful Outcomes: Developing evaluation methods that capture both quantitative and qualitative impacts
Problem-Solving Strategies
  • Used iterative design process with multiple feedback loops
  • Consulted research on cost-effective AT solutions
  • Built sustainability considerations into initial planning
  • Developed mixed-methods evaluation approach

Key Takeaways for Professional Practice

This project reinforced that effective technology integration requires equal attention to pedagogy, people, and processes. The most sophisticated tools will fail without proper training, support, and alignment with learning goals. Moving forward, I will approach educational technology decisions with a more critical eye, considering not just what tools to use, but how to implement them in ways that genuinely promote equity and improve learning outcomes for all students.
