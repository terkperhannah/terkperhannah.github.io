Context & Equity Challenge

I am an educator in Edmonton, Alberta, teaching in a secondary school (Grades 7–12) with a diverse student population. Our community includes multilingual learners (ELL), students with identified and unidentified learning needs, and students from historically marginalized communities (Kiritsis & Manolis, 2024).

My teaching background includes math, biology, and computer science, and I regularly integrate technology into instruction. Over the past four years, my technology use has evolved from simple LMS posting to building interactive virtual classroom extensions that align with Universal Design for Learning principles (CAST, n.d.).

The Digital Divide

A key equity reality in my context is that students use family-purchased personal laptops. Device quality varies widely—from high-performance machines to entry-level devices that struggle with multitasking, a symptom of the "Digital Access Divide" (U.S. Department of Education, 2024).

Inclusive Design

"Everyone has a computer" does not mean everyone has equal access (Miah, 2023). I aim to choose tools that reduce barriers for reading load, processing speed, and executive functioning without relying on high-end hardware.

My Goal

"To use frameworks like SETT, UDL, TPACK, and SAMR to make technology choices that reduce barriers and support student confidence and independence—without relying on tools that only work for students with stronger devices or more resources."

The SETT Framework Analysis

A systematic approach to selecting Assistive Technology (AT) by analyzing the Student, Environment, Tasks, and Tools.

Learner Profile

Target Audience: Math 10C and Math 20-1 (Secondary, Grades 10–12)

Demographics (my context): A diverse class that includes multilingual learners (ELL), students with ADHD/IPPs, and students with varied processing speeds. In my BYOD setting, students use family-purchased laptops, which results in significant variability in device performance. Some students have high-capability machines that can run multiple applications smoothly, while others use basic devices that struggle with multitasking. This variability directly influences how students engage with digital math tools and informs my approach to equitable technology use.

Strengths I can build on

  • Many students are comfortable using basic school tools (Teams, OneNote, browser-based platforms).
  • Students benefit from visual models and worked examples in math.
  • When structure is clear, collaboration is strong (peer tutoring, small-group practice).

This device variability reinforces the importance of examining the student, environment, and task before selecting tools, as emphasized by the SETT Framework (PATINS Project, 2020).

Needs & Challenges

  • Executive functioning: Some students need support with organization, task initiation, and time management (Mosher et al., 2022).
  • Processing + pacing: Students can experience overload when they are expected to listen, copy notes, and solve problems at the same time. Built-in accessibility features and structured practice help reduce this.
  • Device variability: Some students experience lag, crashes, or slower workflow due to entry-level laptops, which can quietly limit participation and confidence (U.S. Department of Education, 2024; Miah, 2023).

What that means for my tool choices

  • Prioritize browser-based tools that run smoothly on low-end devices.
  • Build in low-tech supports (algebra tiles, printed scaffolds) and tactile supports (3D-printed shapes) so learning is not device-dependent.
  • Keep routines consistent across Teams and my class website so students are not “hunting” for materials.

Physical

BYOD classroom. High variability in hardware quality. Limited power outlets. Reliable high-speed Wi-Fi available.

Social

Collaborative seating. Culture of peer support, but some stigma around "special" accommodations. Goal: Universal design.

Instructional

Blended learning model. Microsoft Teams & Google Sites. Emphasis on process over final answers. Fast-paced.

Research-Informed Design Decisions (Module 3)

My approach to adaptive and assistive technology is guided by the principle that tools should remove barriers to learning rather than introduce additional cognitive or logistical demands. Andersen, Jørnø, and Nortvig (2022) emphasize that adaptive technologies are most effective when they personalize learning experiences and support individual pacing without increasing complexity. This aligns with how I use built-in accessibility features and scaffolded digital practice tools in my math classrooms.

In contexts where access to specialized assistive technology is limited, low- and mid-tech solutions can still play a critical role in equitable access. The NEAT (2021) framework reinforced for me that effective support does not require high-tech solutions, but rather an intentional match between the learner’s barrier and the tool provided. This perspective validates my continued use of tactile math tools such as algebra tiles and 3D-printed manipulatives alongside digital supports.

My previous experience using text-to-speech “magic pens” to support struggling readers reflects findings by Klimova and Zamborova (2023), who note that assistive tools are most impactful when they provide immediate access at the point of need. These tools enabled students to engage with grade-level content independently, without waiting for teacher intervention.

Looking ahead, research on AI-driven adaptive learning environments highlights opportunities to further support individualized pacing and reduce cognitive load (Gligorea et al., 2023). While I am cautious about adopting new tools without clear instructional purpose, this is an area I intend to explore thoughtfully as part of my continued professional growth.

Implementation Strategy

Phased Implementation in Secondary Mathematics

This plan is designed for Math 10C and Math 20-1 in a Microsoft-based, BYOD school where students use personally purchased devices with different levels of performance. The focus is equitable access, strong pedagogy, and consistent assessment.

Classroom Actions (Math 10C & 20-1)

  • Set up a predictable flow in Teams + class website: daily notes, worked examples, practice, learning checks.
  • Make materials accessible by default: readable fonts, spacing, high contrast, and compatibility with built-in tools (TTS, dictation, zoom).
  • Plan for BYOD differences: browser-first tools, low-bandwidth options, printable versions when needed.
  • Use math-specific low-tech supports alongside digital tools (algebra tiles for equations; 3D-printed shapes/blocks for surface area and geometry).

Assessment & Equity Moves

  • Reinforce department calculator policy so assessment conditions are consistent across classrooms.
  • Keep summative assessment tools consistent (Microsoft account tools only) and use locked/controlled conditions where required.
  • Use accommodations (ex: extra time) proactively by planning time/space to avoid “next day finishing” issues.
Framework alignment: TPACK (tool choices driven by math outcomes), SETT (needs first), SAMR (Substitution/Augmentation for access).

Math-Specific Integration

  • Build interactive learning checks that give instant feedback for skills like solving linear equations, graphing relations, factoring, radicals, and function interpretation.
  • Include “worked example → try it → feedback → retry” sequences so students practice productively without waiting for marking.
  • Support multilingual learners with vocabulary supports and step-by-step scaffolds built directly into the learning check (not as an afterthought).

Equity & Device-Variation Supports

  • Design tasks that function on basic laptops (no required installs, minimal RAM, fewer tabs needed).
  • Offer alternative formats: paper version, reduced-graphics version, or single-question pages for students who lag or crash.
  • Use formative tech for practice and confidence-building, while keeping summative assessments consistent and secure.
Framework alignment: SAMR (Modification), TPACK (pedagogy-content-tech match), SETT (task-tool fit).

Department Systems (My Role)

  • Maintain shared year plans so grade-level pacing stays aligned and students do not experience “teacher lottery.”
  • Continue common assessments (quizzes, tests, midterms, finals) with consistent conditions and calculator policy enforcement.
  • Create a department “tool standards” page: Microsoft-account tools only, accessibility expectations, and device requirements.

Equity Leadership with CITES

  • Gather student voice data about barriers (device performance, language supports, accessibility features).
  • Use that feedback to adjust tools and design so “access, design, and use” are addressed, not just access.
  • Support staff with quick PD: accessible math materials, consistent assessment accommodations, and tool selection routines.
Framework alignment: CITES (system-level equity), leadership for consistency, ethical tech use.

Bottom line: My long-term goal is for equity supports to be predictable and normal for students, not dependent on which teacher they have or what kind of laptop they can afford.

Module 5

Equity Leadership & Systems Change

Module 5 pushed me to move beyond “What tool helps this student?” and into “What systems do we need so supports are consistent and fair across classes?” As a Mathematics Department Head, I see how quickly equity can slip when every teacher is doing their own thing.

CITES Lens

I use the CITES lens to check whether our technology choices are inclusive by design, and whether they create predictable access for students across classrooms. This matters in a school where students purchase their own devices and experiences can vary.

Course connection: CITES Framework (CAST, 2024).

A Real Consistency Issue I’m Managing

One example is our department’s calculator policy. For high school math, we require students to use the same approved graphing calculator model (and it matches what the school has available as spares). Newer calculators can include internet access or advanced features that create integrity and fairness issues. Some teachers do not see this as a problem, but as department head, I have to ensure the policy is followed consistently so assessment conditions are comparable.

Equity connection: consistent expectations protect fairness across classrooms.

System Practices We Already Use

Our department runs common assessments (quizzes, unit tests, midterms, and finals) and follows a shared year plan. This helps us stay aligned so students have similar pacing and expectations across classes, not just within one room. IT controls internet access, but teachers decide how technology is used day-to-day, so consistency has to be led through clear norms and shared resources.

Next system step I want to lead

  • Create a shared “approved tools” list with accessibility + device requirements.
  • Standardize accessible formatting for digital notes and assessments.
  • Build a quick teacher checklist for equity: access, design, and use.

What I’m still working on

The biggest leadership growth area for me is having tough conversations when “teacher preference” conflicts with equity and consistency. I want to keep building structures that support teacher autonomy while still protecting fairness and access for students across the department.

Module 6

Legal & Ethical Commitments

Module 6 helped me reflect on the tension between compliance and true inclusion. In practice, I am constantly balancing accommodations that are legally and ethically appropriate with the need for fair, secure assessment conditions.

“Fair” vs. “Equal” in Real Assessment Decisions

A common accommodation I support is extra time. I have about 20 students who receive extra time supports, and approximately 4 students with IPPs (often for ADHD). I honour the accommodation, but I also protect assessment integrity. For example, when a test occurs at the end of the day, I do not allow students to finish it the next day. If students go home, they can practice, get help, or access information that changes the conditions of the assessment. This is one of those situations where equity means meeting a diagnosed need, while still keeping the assessment fair for everyone.

How I try to keep the balance

  • Plan extra time proactively (alternate location or earlier start when possible).
  • Use consistent rules across classes for “same-day completion.”
  • Communicate expectations ahead of time so students are not surprised.

Ethical Technology Use in a Microsoft School

At my school, students use their school Microsoft account for the tools we use. That helps with consistency, privacy, and access because we are not asking students to create personal accounts or sign up for random platforms. However, because students purchase their own laptops, equity can still show up in quieter ways when some devices handle multiple programs smoothly and others struggle with performance, storage, or battery life.

My ethical checks before adopting a tool

  • Access: Can students use it through their Microsoft account and on a basic device?
  • Privacy: Does it keep student data inside our school-managed ecosystem?
  • Transparency: Can I clearly explain what students are submitting and who can see it?
  • Fairness: Will the task unintentionally reward students with higher-end hardware?

What this looks like in my classroom

  • I prioritize browser-based options when possible so students are not forced to install heavy software.
  • I build in alternatives (paper, printable, or low-bandwidth versions) so device limitations do not block learning.
  • I keep assessment conditions consistent, especially when accommodations are involved.

Critical Self-Reflection

Examining the journey of designing for equity.

Professional Growth

Through this project, I've moved from viewing accommodations as "exceptions" to "essential design features." I learned that designing for the margins creates a better environment for everyone (Curb Cut Effect). This aligns with the belief that technology requires intentional training (Sesan, 2020).

Addressing Challenges

The biggest obstacle was device variability. I addressed this by strictly filtering for browser-based, lightweight tools. This constraint forced creative solutions, moving from "equal" access to "equitable" classrooms (Smith, 2023).

Connection to Course Goals

This implementation plan directly applies EDU 887 principles by moving beyond "access" to "equity".

The SETT framework provided the structure to stop chasing "shiny tools" and analyze student needs (Zabala, n.d.). This shift defines my future leadership (Kiritsis & Manolis, 2024).
