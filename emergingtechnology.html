1 Context Introduction & Equity Challenge

Educational Context

Setting: STEM-focused junior high and high school (Grades 7–12) in Edmonton, Alberta. The student population is diverse and includes multilingual learners, students with identified and unidentified learning needs, and students from historically marginalized communities.

Technology Context: Students are expected to bring personal laptops that families purchase independently. That creates a real access gap inside the same classroom. Some students have high-performance devices (faster processors, more memory, better battery life) that can run multiple programs smoothly. Others have entry-level devices that struggle with lag, storage limits, and software compatibility. On paper, “everyone has a computer,” but in practice, students do not experience technology access the same way.

Equity Challenge

In my math and robotics classes, a consistent equity challenge is ensuring students can participate meaningfully in learning tasks when they face barriers like reading load, visual-spatial processing demands, sustained attention, executive functioning, and pace differences. These barriers become more visible when tasks assume reliable device performance, quick switching between apps, or consistent access to the same tools and settings.

Why this matters

If the learning task depends on a “strong” laptop (or assumes students already know how to use accessibility features), then the tool becomes another barrier instead of a support. This project focuses on choosing tools intentionally so students can access the math/robotics thinking, not just the technology.

2 Low-Tech to High-Tech Supports in My Practice

In my current school context, we do not have many designated assistive technologies because there is no dedicated funding for those tools. However, every student has a personal computer, and that in itself has expanded what supports I can offer. Even without specialized programs, students can use built-in accessibility features like text-to-speech, speech-to-text, colour-contrast adjustments, font enlargement, and on-screen annotation tools. These low- and mid-tech options may seem very simple; however, they genuinely help students who struggle with decoding, visual processing, or English language proficiency. The NEAT video (2021) explains that low-, mid-, and high-tech tools can all have a place if they directly address a learner’s barrier.

In my previous school, students did not have personal devices, and the budget was very limited. One of the few tools available to support struggling readers was a “magic pen” that read printed text aloud when students ran the pen across the page. Although it was low-tech, it was one of the most effective tools we had because it provided real-time decoding support without a steep learning curve. Klimova and Zamborova (2023) highlight that the impact is greatest at the moment a tool provides access, and that is exactly what those pens did.

Low-tech supports I still use

I rely on algebra tiles to help students visualize solving equations and understand operations more concretely. I also 3D-print shapes and blocks for surface area, which supports students who need tactile, hands-on learning experiences. These tools look “simple,” but they remove meaningful barriers by making abstract concepts more accessible.

How I decide what to use

I start by naming the barrier first (reading load, attention, pace, visual processing, working memory, device limitations), then choose the tool that reduces that barrier without adding new complexity. This is where SETT helps me slow down and match tools to tasks instead of picking technology for convenience.

Andersen et al. (2022) emphasize that adaptive technologies are most useful when they personalize learning and remove obstacles rather than adding layers of complexity. The SETT Framework supports that same idea: consider the Student, Environment, and Tasks before choosing Tools. The PATINS Project (2020) reinforced that alignment piece for me: the tool should fit the task, not the other way around.

Next, I want to explore adaptive digital tools that support pacing and feedback in a realistic way for mixed-capability devices, so the support is consistent for students regardless of what laptop they show up with.

3 Applying the SETT Framework

The SETT Framework (Student, Environment, Tasks, Tools) helps me make decisions that are collaborative and barrier-focused, not tool-first. In this project, I’m using SETT to guide choices for my math and robotics context, especially given the mixed device capabilities in the classroom.

Student
  • Needs may include reading support, pacing support, attention supports, visual-spatial scaffolds.
  • Multilingual learners may need reduced language load without reducing thinking demand.
  • Students benefit when they can choose supports (ex: text-to-speech vs. enlarged text).
Environment
  • Bring-your-own-device setting: wide range of performance and compatibility.
  • Not all students can run the same apps smoothly.
  • Classroom routines must include “how to access supports,” not assume students already know.
Tasks
  • Math tasks: multi-step problem solving, reading word problems, showing reasoning.
  • Robotics tasks: documenting builds, iterating designs, testing code, reflecting.
  • Assessment tasks: demonstrating learning without the tool becoming the gatekeeper.
Tools
  • Built-in accessibility tools (TTS, STT, contrast, zoom, dictation, captions).
  • Low-tech manipulatives (algebra tiles) and 3D-printed models for tactile access.
  • Web-based learning checks designed to run on lower-spec devices.

Design rule I’m using

If a support only works for students with powerful laptops, it is not an equitable support in my setting. Tools have to be realistic for the classroom environment I actually have.

4 Implementation Plan

This plan focuses on supports I can implement consistently in a Grades 7–12 setting, across math and robotics, while accounting for differences in device performance.

Step 1: Identify barriers and baseline routines

I will name the common barriers (reading load, pacing, visual processing, attention/executive functioning, device limitations) and explicitly teach students how to access built-in accessibility tools on their devices.

Step 2: Provide low-tech and digital options for the same goal

For key concepts (ex: solving equations, surface area), students can choose between manipulatives/3D models, guided examples, or digital practice with immediate feedback.

Step 3: Make learning checks lightweight and accessible

Learning checks will be web-based and designed to run smoothly on entry-level devices, with clear navigation, readable fonts, and optional supports (zoom, contrast-friendly layout, simplified page load).

Step 4: Build in reflection and student feedback

Students will reflect briefly on which support helped them (and why) so I can adjust tools and routines. This keeps the focus on “what removes the barrier” instead of “what is the fanciest tool.”

5 Monitoring & Evaluation

Student evidence

Completion rates on practice/learning checks, quality of reasoning shown, and student self-assessment.

Access indicators

Whether students can use supports independently (TTS, zoom, contrast) and whether supports work across devices.

Teacher reflection

What barriers are showing up most often, what tools are effective, and what needs adjustment in routines.

What “success” looks like

More students demonstrate their thinking with fewer technology-related breakdowns, and students can describe which supports help them. If only a few students can access the “best” version of the learning task, the plan needs revision.

6 Self-Reflection

My growth has been moving from seeing technology as “organization” (posting notes and assignments) toward seeing it as “design”: choosing tools and routines that remove barriers. In a bring-your-own-device environment, equity is not just whether students have a device, but whether the learning experience is accessible on the device they actually have.

As a math department head and robotics coach, I also have a leadership responsibility to normalize accessibility supports (not as “special” tools, but as standard options), and to advocate for realistic, inclusive practices that work across classrooms.
