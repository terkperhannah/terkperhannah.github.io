Biology 20 Curriculum

Biology 20 explores how living systems function at both the ecosystem and organism level. Throughout this course, students investigate the flow of energy, the cycling of matter, population change, cellular processes, and the organization of human body systems.

This course is organized around four major units from the Alberta Biology 20 Program of Studies. Click a unit below to explore notes, assignments, labs, and review materials.

Course Units

Unit A: Energy and Matter Exchange in the Biosphere

Explore how energy flows through ecosystems and how matter cycles through the biosphere.

  • Energy flow in ecosystems
  • Trophic levels, food chains, and food webs
  • Ecological pyramids
  • Carbon, oxygen, nitrogen, and phosphorus cycles
  • Biosphere equilibrium and human impact

Unit B: Ecosystems and Population Change

Study ecosystem structure, limiting factors, biodiversity, and how populations change over time.

  • Species, populations, communities, and ecosystems
  • Biotic and abiotic factors
  • Habitats and niches
  • Taxonomy and binomial nomenclature
  • Natural selection and evidence for evolution

Unit C: Photosynthesis and Cellular Respiration

Investigate how cells capture, transform, store, and release energy through essential biochemical processes.

  • Structure and function of chloroplasts
  • Photosynthesis pathways
  • Structure and function of mitochondria
  • Cellular respiration and ATP production
  • Energy transformation in living systems

Unit D: Human Systems

Examine the structure, function, and interactions of major human body systems.

  • Digestive system
  • Respiratory system
  • Circulatory system
  • Excretory system
  • Homeostasis and system interactions

Biology 20 Learning Focus

  • Explain the constant flow of energy through the biosphere and ecosystems
  • Explain the cycling of matter through the biosphere
  • Explain how energy and matter exchange in the biosphere maintains equilibrium
  • Explain that the biosphere is composed of ecosystems with distinctive biotic and abiotic characteristics
  • Explain the mechanisms involved in population change over time
  • Investigate photosynthesis and cellular respiration as complementary biological processes
  • Analyze how structure supports function in living systems
  • Examine the organization and interaction of major human body systems
  • Apply scientific inquiry and laboratory skills to biological investigations
  • Evaluate human impact on ecosystems, biological balance, and sustainability

Biology 20 Attitude Outcomes

In Biology 20, students are also expected to develop attitudes that support responsible scientific learning and application.

Interest in Science

Show curiosity about science-related questions, issues, and possible careers in science.

Mutual Respect

Appreciate that scientific understanding grows through different perspectives and backgrounds.

Scientific Inquiry

Seek evidence, ask questions, and think critically when exploring biological ideas and problems.

Collaboration

Work effectively with others when planning investigations and communicating scientific ideas.

Stewardship

Demonstrate responsibility toward environmental sustainability and the impact of human actions.

Safety

Show concern for safety in labs and investigations, including proper handling and disposal of materials.
